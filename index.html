About

Bridging lived
experience and
rigorous research.

Mohammed Khalid holds a Master of Science in Security Informatics from Johns Hopkins University (GPA: 4.0) and a Bachelor of Science in Information Systems from the University of Maryland, Baltimore County (Magna Cum Laude). His graduate research focused on ransomware risk modeling and the forecasting of cyber threats, culminating in a first-of-its-kind machine learning study on ransomware valuation.

His counter-extremism work spans research, policy, and public engagement. He co-authored a chapter in Oxford University Press's Terrorism, Violent Radicalisation, and Mental Health (2021) and has presented research on online radicalization, extremist digital communications, and deradicalization at institutions including the FBI Behavioral Analysis Unit, the U.S. Department of Defense, the New York City Police Department, Yale University, Georgetown University, and the Washington Institute for Near East Policy.

His professional cybersecurity experience includes privacy risk consulting at Ernst & Young across global clients, FISMA compliance auditing for federal health sector contractors at Guidehouse, and applied research at the Johns Hopkins Information Security Institute and Georgia State University's Evidence-Based Cybersecurity Research Group.

His story — one of the youngest people charged with terrorism in U.S. history, and his subsequent deradicalization — was the subject of a feature profile in The Washington Post. That lived experience, combined with formal academic training, gives him a perspective on radicalization and extremism that is uniquely credible across both practitioner and academic audiences.
